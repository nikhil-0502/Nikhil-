I am a final-year undergraduate at the Madras School of Economics, heading to the MSc Financial Mathematics at the London School of Economics. What caught my attention was seeing measure-theoretic ideas, especially risk-neutral expectations, show up in actual pricing formulas rather than staying abstract.

Since then, I have been building and testing models around that: simulating GBM paths, pricing options with Monte Carlo, and checking how sensitive outputs are to discretisation, volatility, and parameter shifts. Most of this I have implemented in Python and C++, focusing less on getting a number and more on how stable that number actually is.

I am interested in the parts where models stop behaving cleanly, where assumptions start to matter. My current projects span market microstructure (liquidity regimes as Markov chains), credit risk (reconstructing the Jarrow-Lando-Turnbull framework from first principles), financial contagion between the S&P 500 and Nifty 50, and a dynamic welfare analysis of sin taxation using Markov consumption models calibrated to NSSO data.

At Madras School of Economics, I have held seven teaching assistantships covering stochastic processes, game theory, time series analysis, microeconomics, multivariable calculus, and finance. I maintain a CGPA of 9.66 and have been on the Dean's List in every graded semester.

Outside of economics, I am a FIDE-rated chess player who has represented Chennai District at state level. I read widely in the history of economic thought, and I am drawn to the kind of mathematics that surprises you.

Compulsory Courses
ST425Statistical Inference: Principles, Methods and Computation ►
A rigorous graduate-level treatment of statistical inference. Covers the formal principles of likelihood-based inference: maximum likelihood estimation, score functions, Fisher information, and the Cramer-Rao lower bound. Develops Bayesian inference including prior and posterior distributions, conjugate families, and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods (Gibbs sampler, Metropolis-Hastings). Treats bootstrap and resampling methods, model selection (AIC, BIC, cross-validation), and elements of nonparametric and semiparametric inference. Computational implementation throughout in R.
ST436Financial Statistics ►
Covers key statistical methods and data analytic techniques most relevant to finance. Hands-on experience in analysing financial data in R is an essential part of the course. Topics include: obtaining and processing financial data; low- and high-frequency financial time series; ARCH-type models for low-frequency volatilities and their alternatives; predicting equity indices (case study); Markowitz portfolio theory and the Capital Asset Pricing Model; machine learning in financial forecasting; Value at Risk; and simple systematic trading strategies. The course concludes with an extended case study involving predictions of market movements in a virtual trading environment.
ST458Machine Learning in Finance ►
Covers a wide range of modern financial data analytics, bridging advanced concepts in financial statistics to hands-on practice. Builds directly on ST436, extending statistical and machine learning methods to more complex computational problems in finance. Topics include advanced portfolio optimisation, factor models, gross and maximum exposure constraints for large portfolios, algorithmic trading, and deep learning approaches to financial prediction. Consolidation through case studies and problem sets; implemented in R. Core references include Hastie, Tibshirani and Friedman (Elements of Statistical Learning), Dixon, Halperin and Bilokon (Machine Learning in Finance), and Jansen (Machine Learning for Algorithmic Trading).
Mathematical Finance Courses
ST409Stochastic Calculus ►
A rigorous introduction to stochastic processes with emphasis on financial and actuarial applications. Covers martingales and the optional stopping theorem; Poisson processes and their financial applications; Brownian motion, quadratic variation, and the construction of the Ito integral; stochastic differential equations and diffusion processes; Ito's formula and Girsanov's theorem; and the Feynman-Kac representation linking SDEs to PDEs. Applications include option pricing under risk-neutral measure, ruin theory in insurance, and continuous-time interest rate models.
MA415Mathematics of the Black and Scholes Theory ►
Develops the mathematical theory of risk-neutral valuation from first principles. In the binomial tree model, introduces replication, no-arbitrage pricing, and martingale probability measures. Extends to continuous time: derives European contingent claim prices as risk-neutral expectations, pricing formulae for put and call options, and the Black and Scholes PDE via self-financing portfolio arguments. Establishes the connection to the heat equation via Feynman-Kac. PDE methods for pricing barrier options, American options, and path-dependent derivatives. Treats implied volatility surfaces and stochastic volatility models. Extends the framework to foreign exchange markets and currency options.
ST461Mathematics of Market Microstructure ►
Advanced mathematical frameworks for modern market microstructure modelling. Part I covers stochastic processes with and without jumps for order arrival and price formation; the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation and dynamic programming under uncertainty; inventory models and market liquidity; price impact without asymmetric information; optimal execution via the Almgren-Chriss and Obizhaeva-Wang frameworks; and the Avellaneda-Stoikov model of limit order books. Part II treats market equilibrium under asymmetric information in continuous time using stochastic filtering and Kalman filtering: the Kyle model in continuous time and extensions; the Glosten-Milgrom sequential trading model and its continuous-time extension; and the Glosten model of electronic limit order books. Part III addresses current challenges including decentralised exchanges, automated market making, and dark pools.
MA417Computational Methods in Finance ►
Develops computational skills and introduces numerical techniques of central importance to financial engineering. Covers random number generation, Monte Carlo simulation fundamentals, variance reduction techniques (antithetic variates, control variates, importance sampling), and quasi-Monte Carlo methods. Numerical solutions to stochastic differential equations via Euler-Maruyama and Milstein schemes. Finite-difference methods for PDEs arising in finance: explicit and implicit schemes applied to the Black and Scholes PDE, including stability and convergence analysis.
ST463Stochastic Simulation and Calibration ►
Equips students with skills to apply modern statistical and machine learning methods to computational problems in quantitative finance, risk management, and insurance. Covers key aspects of Monte Carlo methods; simulation of stochastic processes; and generative adversarial networks with applications to risk management. Develops generalised linear models as a bridge to deep neural networks, with applications in insurance pricing. Addresses the key challenges of effective calibration of statistical and machine learning models. Concludes with reinforcement learning and applications to hedging in commodity and energy markets via swing option pricing. Core references include Glasserman (Monte Carlo Methods in Financial Engineering) and Sutton and Barto (Reinforcement Learning: An Introduction).
MA435Deep Learning in Finance ►
Introduces computational problems in financial markets and their solution using deep learning and machine learning. Applications include portfolio optimisation, optimal trade execution, pricing and hedging of financial derivatives, and calibration of stochastic volatility models. Theoretical foundations cover empirical risk minimisation, bias-complexity tradeoff, model selection, and validation. Advanced topics include deep learning: feedforward neural networks, universal approximation theorems, stochastic gradient descent, backpropagation, regularisation, recurrent networks, and convolutional architectures. Practical implementation in Python throughout. Key references include Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville (Deep Learning) and Horvath, Muruguza and Tomas (Deep Learning Volatility).
